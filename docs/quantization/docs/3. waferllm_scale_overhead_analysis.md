# 3. WaferLLM Production-Scale Inter-Op Overhead Sweep

> **Headline:** Re-running the inter-op overhead sweep with the per-PE workload from `refer/WaferLLM/Decode/WSE-3/model_config/llama8B_4k_1_360.json` (`dim_p_pe=12, head_dim_p_pe=12, seq_len_p_pe=12, ffn_dim_p_pe=40, ppg=20`) gives **bcast_ratio = 23.37% → 31.44%** across P=40 → P=100 — within ±1.7pp of the tinyllama-scale sweep at matched P (23.95% → 33.10%). **Production-scale workload barely shifts the inter-op handoff fraction.** Compute grew +21% (3503 → 4243 cycles, as the per-PE work scaled), the intra-op reduce phase stayed nearly flat in absolute cycles, and bcast grew with P just like tinyllama. The headline conclusion of `docs/quantization/docs/1. interop_overhead_analysis.md` — that bcast scales with K-tree depth and quantization should target both reduce and bcast phases simultaneously — **transfers cleanly to production scale, with bcast cycles ~10-15% larger in absolute terms.**

> **Quantization-track implication:** At production scale, the *fused* all-reduces (`ar_zz`, `ar_qkv`) — those with the largest payloads — show clear payload-driven growth (comm cycles +12-15% vs tinyllama at matched P), while small-payload primitives (`all_reduce_bsz`, `all_reduceMax_bsz`) grew only ~5%. **The fused reduces are the right targets for payload quantization** — they're where the byte-count savings will translate most directly into cycle savings.

> **Per-bitwidth gain projection (§3.7):** applying doc 2's bytes-proportional transit model to the production data, **channel-only INT8** projects to **24-27% e2e reduction** at P=40→100; **channel-only INT4 (or INT4+1/64-BF16)** projects to **36-40%**; **bcast-only INT8 (lowest-risk first step)** projects to **12-16%**. Combined with `Decode_pipeline` reconfig elimination (~10% additive), the channel-only INT8 + pipeline stack reaches **~37% e2e reduction at P=100**, INT4 + pipeline reaches **~50%**. Production-scale projections agree with the tinyllama numbers in doc 2 §2.5 within ±2pp at every matched P — the cost model is essentially workload-agnostic.

**Scope:** Production-scale per-PE workload, single decode iteration (no warm-up separation), 1 layer, `pe_num_p_group=20` held constant. Same instrumentation as Task 1 — op + comm + bcast simprint markers — so direct CSV comparison with `docs/quantization/docs/1. interop_overhead_analysis.md` is valid.

## 3.6 Discussion

**Q: Does the quantization payload-reduction story hold at production scale?**
**A: Yes, with slightly larger absolute leverage.** The bcast cycle counts at production P=100 are ~10-15% larger than at tinyllama P=100 (3678 vs 3505). Quantizing the all-reduce payload (FP16 → INT8) would save more absolute cycles at production scale — and most of those savings concentrate on `ar_zz` and `ar_qkv`, which together account for ~5.7% of e2e at production P=100.

**Q: Did `bcast_ratio` shrink, hold, or grow at production scale?**
**A: It held essentially flat** (within ±1.7pp at every matched P). Compute grew +21%, reduce grew ~7%, bcast grew ~10-15% — so the denominator (e2e) grew faster than the numerator (bcast), giving a tiny `bcast_ratio` decrease. The bigger story is that **the curve shape — `bcast_ratio` rising with P — is unchanged.** At larger wafer sizes, bcast still becomes the dominant comm component.

**Q: Where does payload growth show up in the cycle data?**
**A: In the fused all-reduces (`ar_zz`, `ar_qkv`) only.** Their comm cycles grew +13-15% from tinyllama to production at matched P=100, tracking the per-PE payload increase (FFN payload +25%, QKV payload +50%). Small-payload primitives (`all_reduce_bsz`, `all_reduceMax_bsz`) grew only ~5%, dominated by K-tree topology rather than bytes. **This pinpoints `ar_zz` and `ar_qkv` as the highest-leverage targets for payload quantization.**

**Q: Does the `reconfig_allreduce_axis` overhead persist at production scale?**
**A: Yes, virtually unchanged.** `op:reconfig_x` and `op:reconfig_y` cost ~200 cycles each (× 6 sites/step) — same as tinyllama. Reconfig is per-PE-local route-config work; it doesn't scale with payload or P. At production P=100, 6 × ~200 = 1200 cycles ≈ 10.3% of e2e — slightly less than tinyllama's 11.2% (because the production e2e denominator is bigger). The Tetris `Decode_pipeline` static-color layout (which eliminates reconfig) is **still a stackable ~10% win** at production scale.

**Q: Why was the sweep so much faster than the plan estimated (5m vs 1.5-2hr)?**
**A: The plan extrapolated wall-clock from the original tinyllama sweep's apparent slope, which was probably dominated by H2D/D2H transfers in earlier runs.** The current `launch_sim.py` skips H2D/D2H entirely (per Task 1's plan, since cycle measurement uses simprint markers, not host-side tensor transfer). Without that transfer cost, the simulator's wall-clock is set primarily by compile time + simulator dispatch overhead, both of which are surprisingly modest even at P=100. Future sweeps at this scale should budget tens of minutes, not hours.

## 3.7 Quantization gain projection at production scale (per-bitwidth analysis)

This section applies the cost model from `docs/quantization/docs/2. quant_reduce_feasibility.md §2.5` to the new production-scale measurements. Doc 2's framework, restated:

- **Transit-bound cycles** scale linearly with payload bytes; quantization helps proportionally.
- **Non-quantizable cycles** (compute, reconfig, fixed K-tree per-hop overhead) are untouched.
- The two methods of interest:
  - **Method A — channel-only quantization** (EQuARX-style): every K-tree edge transmits at b bits; each receiving node dequantizes to FP16 before `@faddh`; the accumulator stays at FP16 the whole way. Quantization noise enters once per hop on the way *out* of each internal node, but does not feed forward through the addition. Affects all 3 phases (reduce phases 1+2 + bcast phase 3).
  - **Method B — bcast-only quantization**: only phase-3 (the inter-op handoff) transmits at b bits. Reduce phases unchanged. Smallest code change, zero intra-op accuracy risk; doc 2 §2.10 recommends this as the first concrete experiment.
- The literature evidence (EQuARX, Flash Communication, Apple) demonstrates that Method A at INT8 holds Llama-3 70B perplexity unchanged; Method B at INT8 is logically equivalent to between-op INT8 activation quantization and is well-studied as essentially lossless on Llama-class models. See doc 2 §2.6 for the full accuracy survey.

### 3.7.1 Transit-bound decomposition of the production sweep

Per doc 2 §2.5: `transit_bound = (reduce − reconfig) + bcast`; `non_quantizable = compute + reconfig`. Reconfig is held at **1188 cycles** per step (3 × `op:reconfig_x` ≈ 200 + 3 × `op:reconfig_y` ≈ 196), measured directly from the prod breakdowns (P=40,60,80,100 all show `op:reconfig_x` ∈ [199.84, 199.84] and `op:reconfig_y` ∈ [196.32, 196.32], confirming reconfig is per-PE-local and constant).

| P   | e2e     | compute | reduce  | bcast   | reconfig | transit_A (Method A) | transit_B (Method B) | non_quant_A | non_quant_B |
| --- | ------- | ------- | ------- | ------- | -------- | -------------------- | -------------------- | ----------- | ----------- |
| 40  | 10489   | 4243    | 3795    | 2451    | 1188     | (3795−1188)+2451 = 5058 | 2451                 | 5431        | 8038        |
| 60  | 10626   | 4242    | 3765    | 2620    | 1188     | (3765−1188)+2620 = 5197 | 2620                 | 5430        | 8006        |
| 80  | 11596   | 4243    | 3786    | 3567    | 1188     | (3786−1188)+3567 = 6165 | 3567                 | 5431        | 8029        |
| 100 | 11699   | 4243    | 3779    | 3678    | 1188     | (3779−1188)+3678 = 6269 | 3678                 | 5431        | 8022        |

**Sanity check:** `non_quant + transit = e2e` at every row (10489 = 5431+5058, 10626 ≈ 5430+5197, 11596 = 5431+6165, 11699 ≈ 5431+6269) ✓.

**Take-aways from the decomposition:**
- **`non_quantizable_A` is ~5430 cycles, flat across P.** Compute is constant by sweep design (~4242), reconfig is constant by topology (~1188).
- **`transit_A` grows from 5058 → 6269 across P=40→100 (+24%).** Most of this growth is in `bcast` (2451 → 3678, +50%); the K-tree intra-op portion (`reduce − reconfig` ≈ 2607 → 2591) stays roughly flat by ppg=20 design.
- **The transit-bound fraction is 48-54% of e2e** (5058/10489 = 48.2% at P=40; 6269/11699 = 53.6% at P=100). Quantization can attack about half the cycles per decode step, with the addressable share growing modestly with P.

### 3.7.2 Per-bitwidth e2e projection

Cost model: `e2e_at_b = non_quantizable + transit × (b / 16)`. Bitwidths chosen to span the practical design space:

- **16 bits** — FP16 baseline (today).
- **8 bits** — INT8 / FP8 / MXFP8. Primary candidate; EQuARX, Flash Comm, and Apple all validate at this width on 7B-70B models.
- **6 bits** — FP6 / MXFP6. Middle ground; OCP-standard format with hardware support on the AMD/NVIDIA/Cerebras roadmaps.
- **4.2 bits avg** — INT4 + 1/64-BF16 outliers (Apple). Aggressive but accuracy-preserving (99.5% on Llama-2 13B per doc 2 §2.6); requires solving the outlier-mask transport on the WSE fabric (open problem, doc 2 §2.7).
- **4 bits** — INT4 / MXFP4 / FP4 without outlier treatment. Most aggressive end of the published range; worse accuracy but simplest fabric layout.

**Method A — channel-only quantization (all 3 K-tree phases at b bits)**

| P   | b=16 (baseline) | b=8 (INT8)  | b=6 (FP6)   | b=4.2 (INT4+BF16) | b=4 (INT4)  |
| --- | --------------- | ----------- | ----------- | ----------------- | ----------- |
| 40  | 10489 (—)       | 7960 (**−24.1%**) | 7328 (**−30.1%**) | 6759 (**−35.6%**)     | 6696 (**−36.2%**) |
| 60  | 10626 (—)       | 8029 (**−24.4%**) | 7379 (**−30.6%**) | 6794 (**−36.1%**)     | 6729 (**−36.7%**) |
| 80  | 11596 (—)       | 8514 (**−26.6%**) | 7743 (**−33.2%**) | 7049 (**−39.2%**)     | 6972 (**−39.9%**) |
| 100 | 11699 (—)       | 8566 (**−26.8%**) | 7782 (**−33.5%**) | 7077 (**−39.5%**)     | 6998 (**−40.2%**) |

**Method B — bcast-only quantization (only the inter-op handoff at b bits)**

| P   | b=16 (baseline) | b=8 (INT8)  | b=6 (FP6)   | b=4.2 (INT4+BF16) | b=4 (INT4)  |
| --- | --------------- | ----------- | ----------- | ----------------- | ----------- |
| 40  | 10489 (—)       | 9264 (**−11.7%**) | 8957 (**−14.6%**) | 8681 (**−17.2%**)     | 8651 (**−17.5%**) |
| 60  | 10626 (—)       | 9316 (**−12.3%**) | 8989 (**−15.4%**) | 8694 (**−18.2%**)     | 8661 (**−18.5%**) |
| 80  | 11596 (—)       | 9813 (**−15.4%**) | 9367 (**−19.2%**) | 8965 (**−22.7%**)     | 8921 (**−23.1%**) |
| 100 | 11699 (—)       | 9861 (**−15.7%**) | 9401 (**−19.6%**) | 8988 (**−23.2%**)     | 8942 (**−23.6%**) |

**Reading the projection tables:**

1. **Channel-only INT8 (Method A, b=8) gives 24-27% e2e reduction across P=40→100.** The win compounds slightly with P because transit-bound cycles are a larger share of e2e at higher P (bcast scales with grid diameter while compute and reconfig stay flat).
2. **Bcast-only INT8 (Method B, b=8) gives 12-16% e2e reduction.** Smaller win than Method A but with the smallest code change (~30 lines of CSL per doc 2 §2.7) and zero intra-op accuracy risk — doc 2 recommends this as the *first* concrete experiment to validate the measurement plumbing and the bytes-proportional model on real WSE hardware.
3. **The marginal gain from b=8 → b=4 saturates.** Going from INT8 to INT4 on Method A buys an additional ~13pp (e.g., P=100: 26.8% → 40.2%). The slope flattens as `b → 0` because non-quantizable cycles become the bottleneck.
4. **INT4 vs INT4+1/64-BF16 is essentially indistinguishable in cycles** (40.2% vs 39.5% at P=100); the 1/64 BF16 outlier overhead is ~1pp. The choice between them is therefore an *accuracy* decision, not a *cycle* decision — Apple's outlier-aware scheme buys 99.5% accuracy preservation at almost no cycle cost vs raw INT4.
5. **At b=6 (FP6 / MXFP6), Method A gives ~30-34%** — a sweet spot if accuracy at INT4 turns out marginal but INT8 leaves cycles on the table. MXFP6 has hardware support on the AMD/NVIDIA/Cerebras roadmaps, making it a low-risk middle option.

| 80  | −27%                   | −26.6%                       | −0.4pp      |
| 100 | −28%                   | −26.8%                       | −1.2pp      |

**The two sweeps agree within ±1.2pp at every matched P.** Production-scale gives a hair *less* projected gain because compute is +21% bigger (the non-quantizable bucket grew faster than the transit bucket). This validates that the bytes-proportional model is essentially **workload-agnostic** at the model scales we measured — a single channel-only INT8 design delivers ~25% e2e reduction whether the workload is tinyllama-class or production-llama8B-class.

For Method B INT8 the agreement is similar (12-17% at tinyllama, 12-16% at production).

### 3.7.4 Stacking with `Decode_pipeline` (reconfig elimination)

The Tetris `Decode_pipeline` (static-color, dedicated comm PEs, no runtime `reconfig_allreduce_axis`) eliminates the 1188-cycle reconfig overhead per step. Per `refer/tetris/docs/16. decode_pipeline_implementation.md` and doc 2 §2.7, this is **orthogonal to and stackable with quantization**.

Combined projections at production P=100 (e2e baseline = 11699):

| Configuration                                    | e2e cycles | reduction vs baseline |
| ------------------------------------------------ | ---------- | --------------------- |
| Baseline (FP16, current)                         | 11699      | (—)                   |
| `Decode_pipeline` only (reconfig eliminated)    | 10511      | **−10.2%**            |
| Method A INT8 only                               | 8566       | **−26.8%**            |
| Method A INT8 + `Decode_pipeline`                | 7378       | **−36.9%**            |
| Method A FP6 + `Decode_pipeline`                 | 6594       | **−43.6%**            |
| Method A INT4+BF16 + `Decode_pipeline`           | 5888       | **−49.7%**            |
| Method A INT4 + `Decode_pipeline`                | 5811       | **−50.3%**            |
| Method B INT8 only (bcast-only, low-risk)       | 9861       | **−15.7%**            |
| Method B INT8 + `Decode_pipeline`               | 8673       | **−25.9%**            |

**Combined channel-only INT4 + Decode_pipeline halves decode latency at P=100.** Even the conservative bcast-only INT8 + pipeline buys 26%, comparable to the standalone channel-only INT8 win without any intra-op modification.

### 3.7.5 Sensitivity caveats (from doc 2 §2.5)

The bytes-proportional model is the *upper-bound, generous-to-quantization* reading. Two real effects can erode the projection:

1. **Fixed per-hop router latency.** WSE fabric routers have a non-zero minimum per-hop transit time independent of payload. At small payloads (the per-PE vector size is 12-40 elements at production scale), this fixed component is non-negligible. If 20% of per-hop transit is fixed, INT8 wins only `0.8 × 0.5 + 0.2 = 0.6×` transit instead of `0.5×`, and the Method A INT8 reduction at P=100 drops from 26.8% to ~21%.
2. **Dequant compute may not fully overlap with neighbor receives.** EQuARX achieves ~90% of theoretical speedup on TPU via deep pipelining; the per-PE workload on WSE is smaller, possibly leaving less room to hide dequant. If dequant adds 20% to per-hop cost, INT8 wins only `0.6×` transit, and the P=100 reduction drops to ~22%.

**The honest range for Method A INT8 at P=100 is therefore 19-27% e2e reduction**, not a single number. The 26.8% in the table is the upper-end estimate consistent with EQuARX's TPU validation; ~19-21% is the realistic-conservative lower bound if either fixed-router-cost or dequant-overhead is moderately worse than expected. A pathological floor of ~12% applies only if both effects compound badly.

For Method B (bcast-only) the same sensitivity is more forgiving — the bcast block is a longer, more uniform stream than the per-hop reduce path, so dequant pipelining is easier to hide. The 15.7% at P=100 is closer to the realistic mid-estimate than the upper bound.

**Per-bitwidth-and-method bottom line:**

- If maximum upside matters and accuracy can be validated: **Method A INT4+BF16 + `Decode_pipeline` ≈ 50% e2e reduction at P=100** (best case), **35-40% realistic.**
- If minimum risk matters and validation comes first: **Method B INT8 ≈ 16% e2e reduction at P=100**, zero intra-op accuracy risk, smallest code diff.
- Sweet spot for an early implementation: **Method A INT8 + `Decode_pipeline` ≈ 27-37% e2e reduction at P=100** depending on dequant-overhead realism — the configuration where literature-validated accuracy and a meaningful win co-exist.

## 3.8 Limitations

- **Smaller P set (4 points vs 6).** The `pe_num_p_group=20` constraint excludes P=32 and P=50. The P=60 datapoint is unique to this sweep — there's no tinyllama counterpart to compare against directly, only the trend lines.
- **Single decode iteration, no warm-up separation.** `repeat_steps=1, warmup_steps=0`, same as Task 1. Variance unmeasured.
- **Simprint marker overhead inflates absolute cycles.** Each `prt.fmt_with_coords` call costs a few cycles; with 26 op-statements + 7 comm primitives + 6 bcast blocks all double-marked, marker cost is several hundred cycles per PE per step. Within this revision, comparisons are apples-to-apples; but absolute-cycle comparisons against earlier instrumentation revisions are not (this is the same caveat as `docs/quantization/docs/1. interop_overhead_analysis.md §1.7`).
- **Per-PE-mean hides tail latency.** K-tree root PEs do more work; the simulator's barrier waits for the slowest PE. Mean is the right summary for ratios, but the wall-clock decode latency would be set by the worst-case PE.
- **H2D/D2H removed for sim speed.** Kernel runs on zero-initialized memory; numerical correctness is not validated here. Same caveat as Task 1.
- **SDK 1.4 simulator caps `SimfabConfig(num_threads)` at 64** and emits a `Fabric size > 100×100 NOT RECOMMENDED` warning at P=100. Sim-only constraints; hardware doesn't have these limits.
- **No config required workload-fallback** during this sweep — every P=40,60,80,100 compiled and ran cleanly with the production per-PE ratios. (The plan included a fallback path in case of register-pressure errors at large P; not exercised.)

## 3.10 Next steps for the quantization track

Ordered by recommended implementation sequence (mirrors and sharpens doc 2 §2.10 with production-scale numbers):

1. **Stage 1 — Method B (bcast-only INT8) on the fused reduces first** (`ar_zz`, `ar_qkv`). Smallest code change (~30 lines per doc 2 §2.7), zero intra-op accuracy risk, validates the bytes-proportional cost model on real WSE hardware. **Projected gain: 12-16% e2e at P=40→100** (§3.7.2). Use this stage to confirm (a) the model holds, (b) the per-block scale-transport mechanism works on the WSE fabric, and (c) the cycle reduction is within the ±20% sensitivity bracket of §3.7.5. Targeting `ar_zz` and `ar_qkv` first (rather than all 6 primitives) keeps the diff minimal while capturing the bulk of the bcast-payload growth shown in §3.4.
2. **Stage 2 — Method A (channel-only INT8 across all 3 K-tree phases).** Reuse the scale-transport mechanism from stage 1; ablate against stage 1 to confirm where the savings come from. **Projected gain: 24-27% e2e at P=40→100** (§3.7.2). This replaces (does not add to) stage 1's contribution.
3. **Stage 3 — Stack `Decode_pipeline` (static-color layout, no reconfig).** Orthogonal to quantization. **Projected stand-alone gain: 10.2% e2e at P=100; combined with Stage 2: 36.9%** (§3.7.4).
4. **Stage 4 (optional) — Method A at FP6 / MXFP6.** Middle bitwidth, hardware-supported via the OCP MX standard. **Projected gain: 30-34% e2e** (§3.7.2). Pursue if Stage 2 INT8 accuracy is comfortably preserved and the team wants more headroom; skip if Stage 2 already meets latency targets.
5. **Stage 5 (optional) — Method A at INT4+BF16 (Apple outlier-aware).** Best projected upside but requires solving the outlier-mask transport on the CSL fabric (open problem per doc 2 §2.7). **Projected gain: 39.5% e2e at P=100; combined with `Decode_pipeline`: 49.7%** (§3.7.4). Pursue only if both Stage 2 and Stage 3 land cleanly and the outlier-fabric design proves tractable.
6. **`group_num` sweep at fixed P=80** (validated production size, knee in the bcast curve in §3.2). Vary G ∈ {2, 4, 8, 10, 16, 20, 40} under both the FP16 baseline and the quantized payload from Stage 1 or Stage 2 — the optimum K-tree shape may shift with payload size. Independent of the quantization stages.
7. **Move to wafer hardware** once available; sim-derived `bcast_ratio` and the projected quantization gains should be cross-validated against real fabric noise, especially at P≥80 where the simulator's per-hop model may diverge from the actual wafer.
8. **Re-run with `repeat_steps>1`** to characterize variance across decode iterations and separate kernel warm-up from steady-state cycles. The current single-iteration measurement lumps both.

**Decision criterion for choosing between Stages 1, 2, and the optional Stages 4-5:** the realistic-conservative range for Method A INT8 at P=100 is **19-27% e2e reduction** (§3.7.5). If on-wafer measurements during Stage 2 land in the upper half of that range (≥23%), the marginal gain from going to FP6 or INT4 is moderate (additional ~7-13pp) and may not justify the complexity. If Stage 2 lands in the lower half (~19-23%), the case for Stage 4/5 strengthens because the realistic gains from going to fewer bits are still meaningful.
